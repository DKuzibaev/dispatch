In 1985, a median-income American household needed to spend roughly three times its annual income to buy a median-priced home. Today, that ratio is eight to one. In London, Sydney, and Vancouver, it is worse. In some neighborhoods of those cities, a median household would need to save for decades to afford a down payment.

This is not a market correction in progress. It is a structural transformation of who can build wealth through homeownership — and who cannot.

## How We Got Here

The causes of the housing crisis are well understood, even if the political will to address them remains elusive. Restrictive zoning in desirable areas has constrained supply for decades. NIMBYism — the organized resistance of existing homeowners to new development — has successfully blocked density in the places where people most want to live. The financialization of housing, accelerated after 2008, turned homes into investment assets, attracting capital that further inflated prices.

Low interest rates throughout the 2010s supercharged the dynamic. When rates rose sharply in 2022 and 2023, buyers were squeezed from both sides — prices had already risen dramatically, and now borrowing costs had as well.

## The Generational Divide

The crisis is not evenly distributed. Homeowners who bought before 2015 have seen their wealth increase dramatically. Those who didn't — largely younger people, renters, and those without family wealth to draw on — have been locked out of the primary wealth-building mechanism that previous generations relied upon.

This divide is increasingly visible in polling data. Younger voters in the UK, Canada, Australia, and the United States consistently rank housing affordability as a top political priority. Older homeowners, whose financial interests are tied to high property values, often oppose the policy changes — particularly increased housing supply — that would most directly address the crisis.

> "We built a system where existing homeowners have every incentive to block the housing younger people need. That's not a market failure. It's a policy choice." — Economist Alain Bertaud

## What Would Actually Help

The policy solutions are not mysterious. More housing supply — particularly dense, transit-accessible housing in high-demand areas — is the most direct intervention. Zoning reform, expedited permitting, and state-level overrides of restrictive local zoning have all shown results in jurisdictions that have tried them.

What has been missing is political will. That may be changing. As younger cohorts represent a larger share of the electorate, politicians are beginning to calculate that the math has shifted. Several states have passed significant zoning reform in recent years. The question is whether the pace of change can match the scale of the problem.
