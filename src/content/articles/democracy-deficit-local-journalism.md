When the Youngstown Vindicator closed in 2019 after 150 years of publication, it became a symbol of the collapse of local news in America. By then, more than 1,800 local papers had already closed since 2004. The pace has accelerated since. Researchers now estimate that one-third of American counties have no dedicated local news coverage at all.

The political consequences of this shift are the subject of a growing body of research that paints a troubling picture.

## What the Research Shows

A study published in the *Journal of Political Economy* found that communities that lost their local newspaper saw measurable increases in municipal borrowing costs — a proxy for reduced accountability and oversight of local government finances. Another study found that voter turnout in local elections dropped significantly in areas that lost local coverage.

Perhaps most strikingly, researchers at the University of Chicago found that corruption convictions of local officials were significantly lower in news deserts — not because corruption had decreased, but because there was no one left to investigate and report it.

## The Structural Problem

The collapse of local news is fundamentally an economic story. The classified advertising revenue that sustained local papers for a century migrated to Craigslist and then to platform marketplaces. Display advertising followed audiences to social media. The business model never recovered.

What has replaced local newspapers is mostly insufficient. Digital-native local news startups have emerged in some markets, but they remain chronically underfunded and struggle to cover the full range of civic life. National outlets have expanded, but they cannot replicate the granular accountability that comes from reporters who have covered the same beat for years.

## A Structural Fix?

The solutions being proposed range from tax credits for local news subscriptions to public funding models similar to those used for public broadcasting. Some researchers have advocated for treating local journalism as public infrastructure, worthy of direct government support — a proposal that raises its own concerns about editorial independence.

What seems clear is that the market alone will not solve this problem. The question is whether the political will exists to treat the collapse of local journalism as the civic emergency it actually is.
