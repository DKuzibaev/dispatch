On a Tuesday morning at the Chicago Public Library's main branch, the chairs are full by 9 a.m. Not with readers browsing bestsellers, though some of those are here too. The majority are people using the library's free Wi-Fi and computer terminals — job seekers, students, recent immigrants navigating paperwork, freelancers who can't afford co-working space.

The public library has always been many things to many people. But over the past decade, as its resources have shrunk and its political environment has become more fraught, the gap between what libraries are asked to do and what they can actually sustain has grown precarious.

## The Funding Squeeze

Public library funding in the United States is overwhelmingly local — drawn from property taxes and municipal budgets that have faced relentless pressure since the 2008 financial crisis. A 2024 survey by the American Library Association found that nearly 60 percent of public libraries reported flat or declining budgets over the previous three years, even as demand for their services increased.

The squeeze shows up in staffing, hours, and collections. Many branches have reduced open hours. Some have eliminated children's programming. A growing number rely on volunteers to fill gaps left by unfilled staff positions.

## The Political Battlefield

Libraries have also become unexpected frontlines in the culture wars. Book challenges and removal requests reached a record high in 2023, according to ALA data, with a significant portion targeting titles dealing with race, sexuality, and gender identity.

Some state legislatures have moved to restrict library acquisitions or penalize librarians for providing access to challenged materials. In a handful of counties, local governments have defunded libraries entirely rather than comply with federal anti-discrimination requirements.

> "The library is the last truly public space. No consumption required, no ticket needed. That's exactly why it's under pressure." — Urban planner Jane Hollis

## What Libraries Are Becoming

Despite the pressures, many libraries are adapting in ways that deepen rather than diminish their role. Tool libraries, seed libraries, recording studios, and maker spaces have become common amenities. Social workers are embedded in branches in dozens of cities, providing services that no other public institution offers at scale.

The question is whether these adaptations can be sustained without a fundamental rethinking of how the institution is funded and protected. The answer may depend on whether Americans still believe in the idea of something that belongs to everyone.
