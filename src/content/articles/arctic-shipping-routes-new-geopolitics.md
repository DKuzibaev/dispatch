The Northwest Passage, long the stuff of explorers' dreams and sailors' nightmares, is becoming a commercial reality. Last summer, a record 42 vessels navigated the Canadian Arctic route — more than in the previous decade combined. The Northern Sea Route along Russia's coast saw even more dramatic growth.

The cause is no mystery: the Arctic is warming four times faster than the rest of the planet. What was once year-round ice pack is now seasonal, and in some projections, the Arctic Ocean could see its first ice-free summer before 2035.

## The Commercial Case

For global shipping companies, the arithmetic is compelling. A voyage from Shanghai to Hamburg via the Northern Sea Route is roughly 7,000 kilometers shorter than the Suez Canal route — saving two weeks of sailing time and significant fuel costs. Container shipping giant Maersk has already commissioned ice-class vessels. Chinese state companies have invested billions in Russian Arctic port infrastructure.

## Russia's Sovereign Claim

But the geopolitics are treacherous. Russia controls the longest stretch of Arctic coastline and has made clear that it views the Northern Sea Route as its sovereign waterway — a claim that NATO allies dispute under international maritime law. Moscow charges access fees, requires Russian pilots aboard foreign vessels, and has recently deployed new military infrastructure along the route.

China's position is characteristically strategic. Beijing has declared itself a "near-Arctic state" and published an Arctic Policy white paper outlining its interest in what it calls the "Polar Silk Road."

## The American Gap

For the United States and its allies, the new Arctic reality presents uncomfortable choices. The U.S. Coast Guard has long acknowledged a critical shortage of icebreakers.

- Russia operates more than **40 icebreakers**
- The United States has **two** operational heavy icebreakers
- Building more takes years and enormous expense

The Arctic's transformation is accelerating faster than policy can adapt. What happens in those waters over the next decade will have consequences for global trade, military balance, and resource competition that are only beginning to come into focus.
